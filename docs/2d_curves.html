Visualizing 2D space-filling curves

Peano curve

The earliest example of a space filling curve is the Peano curve, discovered by Giuseppe Peano in 1890. The context for its construction is quite interesting. In 1874, Georg Cantor published the paper that started set theory, in which he proved that there are no bijections between the integers and the real numbers. This means that while integers are countably inginite, the real numbers are of a higher class of infinity. Cantor also showed that the sets $[0, 1]$ and $[0, 1] \times [0, 1]$ have the same cardinality. This motivated Peano to construct an explicit surjection from $[0, 1]$ to $[0, 1] \times [0, 1]$, which is the space-filling Peano curve.

Hilbert curve

Perhaps the most famous space-filling curve, discovered by the even more famous German mathematician David Hilbert, the Hilbert curve was constructed as a simplified variant of the Peano curve. This is also the most widely used curve for all sorts of applications in computer science.

Z-order curve

The Z-order curve is also known as the Lebesgue curve or the Morton curve. It can be constructed by interleving binary coordinate values. That is, in a discrete coordinate, represented in binary, the z-order curve is constructed by mapping points in space, say $(b000, b111)$ to the point $b010101$. Conversely, the point $b101011$ is mapped to the point $(b111, b001)$. It is also what you get from a depth-first traversal of a quadtree.

Gosper curve

Named after Bill Gosper, the Gosper curve notably does not map to the $[0, 1] \times [0 ,1]$ square. Instead, the image of the curve is called the Gosper island, and it actually tiles $\mathbb{R}^{2}$. In the animation, the order 1 curve is left faintly in the background to better demonstrate how the copies fit together.

Dragon curve

The Dragon curve is another curve that doesn't map to the $[0, 1] \times [0, 1]$ square. The dragon curve turns up a lot when talking about self-similar fractals, not just space-filling curves. Noteably, the dragon curve can be generated by repeatedly folding a strip of paper in half, then unfolding it. When unfolding, all the creases are kept at a 90 degree angle. More precisely, the straight paper strip represents the first order curve, while the $n$-th order curve is obtained by folding the strip in half $n-1$ times, then unfolding to 90 degree angles.
